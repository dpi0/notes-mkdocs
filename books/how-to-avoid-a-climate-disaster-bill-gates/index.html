    NOTES¶

    Chapter 1: Why Zero?¶

    NOTES¶

    Most climate change reports use the Celsius scale for reporting temperature changes. I’ll follow that practice in this book, because that’s what you’ll see in most news reports. To get an idea of a temperature change in Fahrenheit that is accurate enough for most purposes, you can just double the Celsius number and remember that your estimate is a little high. Since most Americans think more naturally in Fahrenheit, I’ll use that scale when I’m referring to daily temperatures. *2 Here’s the math. Recent models suggest that the cost of climate change in 2030 will likely be between 0.85 percent and 1.5 percent of America’s GDP per year. Meanwhile, current estimates for the cost of COVID-19 to the United States this year range between 7 percent and 10 percent of GDP. If we assume that a similar disruption happens once every 10 years, that’s an average annual cost of 0.7 percent to 1 percent of GDP—roughly equivalent to the projected damage from climate change.

    1. what does he mean by going to 0 emissions?¶

    In preindustrial times—before the mid-18th century or so—the earth’s carbon cycle was probably roughly in balance, plants and other things absorbed about as much carbon dioxide as was emitted. But then we started burning fossil fuels adding extra gases. “getting to zero” doesn’t actually mean “zero.” It means “near net zero.” bigger the reduction, the bigger the benefit, so a 50 percent drop in emissions wouldn’t stop the rise in temperature; it would only slow things down at some point we’ll not only need to stop adding more gases but actually need to start removing some of the gases

    2. small rise in temp consequences (1.1)¶

    In climate terms, a change of just a few degrees is a big deal. IPCC reports some uncertainty about how much and how quickly the temperature will go up and exactly what effect these higher temperatures will have. One problem is that computer models are far from perfect as climate is way too complex to be modeled accurately rn. We don’t know precisely what will happen but it will be bad for sure.

    Storms¶

    climate change is making storms wetter and increasing the number of intense ones. Scientists are still debating whether storms are happening more often because of the heat, but they appear to be getting more powerful in general.

    Soil Moisture¶

    By the 2100, soils in the southwestern United States will have 10 percent to 20 percent less moisture, and the risk of drought there will go up by at least 20 percent.

    Wildfires¶

    More frequent and destructive wildfires. Warm air absorbs moisture from plants and soil, leaving everything more prone to burning.

    Sea Levels¶

    sea levels will go up. This is partly because polar ice is melting, and partly because seawater expands when it gets warmer. most likely, a few feet by 2100

    Plants and Animals being affected¶

    According to research cited by the IPCC, a rise of 2 degrees Celsius would cut the geographic range of vertebrates by 8 percent, plants by 16 percent, and insects by 18 percent. mosquitoes will start living in new places (they like it humid, and they’ll move from areas that dry out to ones that become more humid)

    Our Food¶

    yields could go up, but in most places they’ll drop, by anywhere from a few percentage points to as much as 50 percent. sub-Saharan Africa, farmers could see the growing season shrink by 20 percent cut southern Europe’s production of wheat and corn in half by mid-century food prices could rise by 20 percent or more Extreme droughts in China—whose agricultural system provides wheat, rice, and corn Extra heat won’t be good for the animals we eat and get milk from; it will make them less productive and more prone to dying young fish and other sea life are moving to different waters, or simply dying off.

    Heatstroke¶

    Heatstroke as it is linked to the humidity, most in jeopardy—the Persian Gulf, South Asia, and parts of China

    Farmer Examples¶

    He gives example of a farmer in Mid US, and effects on him might’ve seen 33 days a year when the temperature hit 90; now it happens 65 or 70 times a year When you were a kid, you could expect around 25 inches a year; now it might be as little as 22 or as much as 29 a powerful storm strikes without warning your parents would’ve called a hundred-year flood but now once a decade could sell your cattle to make up for the loss, but all your cattle feed has been swept away too stored grain is soaked so thoroughly that it rots waters recede, and you can see that the nearby roads, bridges, and rail lines are now unusable

    Now an example from rural india heat waves have become so common that your village is becoming unlivable several days in a row over 120 degrees monsoons have flooded other parts of the country, your community has received far less rain sent your oldest son to work in a big city hundreds of miles away because you couldn’t afford to feed him try to survive on the farm you know, or abandon the land and move to a more urban area where you might make a living

    3. about greenhouse gases & carbon equivalents¶

    we use carbon dioxide equivalents CO2e to account for the fact that some gases trap more heat than carbon dioxide but don’t stay around as long. methane is much worse than co2, 120 times more warming, but doesn’t stay around as long as co2

    4. how does greenhouse gases trap heat? (1.2)¶

    Sunlight passes right through most greenhouse gases without getting absorbed. Most of it reaches the earth and warms up the planet reflects and some of this energy is emitted in just the right range of wavelengths to get absorbed by greenhouse gases. co2 and methane are, have the right structure to absorb this.

    5. damage by an analogy of COVID-19 & 2 solutions¶

    by mid-century, climate change could be just as deadly as COVID-19, and by 2100 it could be five times as deadly economic damage caused by climate change will likely be as bad as having a COVID-sized pandemic every 10 years large sections of the earth’s permanently frozen ground (called permafrost) gets warm enough to melt and releases the huge amounts of greenhouse gases, mostly methane, that are trapped there

    1. Adaptation minimize the impact of the changes that are already here and that we know are coming (later in chapter 9)
    2. Mitigation stop adding greenhouse gases to the atmosphere, rich countries should go first and give solutions to developing world

    Afterword: Climate Change and Covid-19¶

    1. We need international cooperation.¶

    The phrase “we have to work together” If rich countries worry only about lowering their own emissions and don’t work to make clean technologies practical for everyone, we’ll never get to zero

    2. We need to let science, many different sciences—guide our efforts¶

    climate science tells us why we need to change course but not how to do it. For that, we must draw on engineering, physics, environmental science, economics, and more.

    3. Our solutions should meet the needs of the people who are hardest hit¶

    in 2020, vaccination rates dropped to levels last seen in the 1990s. We lost 25 years of progress in about 25 weeks. Rich nations more they invest in strengthening health systems around the world, the more prepared we will be for the next pandemic In the same way, we need to plan for a just transition to a zero-emissions future rich ones will need to acknowledge that the energy transition will be disruptive for the communities that rely on today’s energy systems significant portion of the working class economy will be affected, and there should be a transition plan in place for those communities

    4. We can do the things that will spark innovation to avoid a climate disaster¶

    By investing in clean-energy research and development—R&D—governments can promote economic recovery that also helps reduce emissions govt can help clean-energy companies grow by adopting policies that reduce the Green Premiums and make it easier for green products to compete with their fossil-based competitors
